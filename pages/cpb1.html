Eating the Soul of the Place

Food isn’t just something we eat; it’s a language of memory and a way to witness the soul of a place.

My mother missed the taste of home,
Oaxaca lived quietly in her bones.
She found it in a quiet corner
where walls were painted with memory.

She ordered mole, tamales, dreams,
then pointed at something near the bottom.
“Chapulines,” she smiled,
and I felt my courage shrink.

Crunchy legs with chili lime
stared back like tiny dares.
She popped one into her mouth
while her eyes lit up with joy.

I followed, breath caught,
teeth meeting roasted spice.
The flavor was oddly warm—
like peanuts, like fear, like trust.

The second bite came easier.
Somewhere between lime and toasted corn,
I tasted the place she came from.
This was not just food.

It was a story
served on a ceramic plate,
a memory folded in masa,
a conversation I never knew I needed.

Now, when I think of travel,
I think of meals that carry memory,
of flavors that speak in unfamiliar tones,
and the quiet bravery it takes to listen.
